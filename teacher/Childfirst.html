Child First Authority, Inc.

Baltimore, MD

Child First Authority (CFA) provides funding to (1) organize high quality, after school and in-school programs in Baltimore City Public Schools, (2) organize parents, school staff, and community partners to act on their vision of a brighter future for BCPS children. CFA was established in 1996 in response to a community organizing campaign by its founding partner BUILD (Baltimoreans United in Leadership Development). Hundreds of citizens expressed the need for safe space, academic achievement, and opportunity for children in Baltimore City Schools.

Child First Art Core

Background: Baltimore has a thriving visual and performing arts scene but many Baltimore City Schools lack resources to provide high quality arts programs to students on a daily basis. Child First Art Core was created to provide art resources to students in Child First partner schools and link the arts community to city schools. Art Core teaching artists are local, professional artists who want to create artwork with students in Child First schools. The role of CFA partner schools is to support teaching artists with necessary resources and skills to work with elementary/middle school students and successfully create quality artwork to display in Child First annual art show.

Purpose: Art Core teaching artists will use an art medium to explore age appropriate topics and community issues in Child First partner schools. Art Core provides youth an opportunity to express their ideas and concerns by exploring creative artistic mediums such as music, poetry, performance, video, drawing, sculpture, and design. The process of creating art will increase student leadership, communication skills, creative thinking, public speaking, teambuilding skills, social awareness and self-confidence.

Curriculum Outline

Overview of Program: What makes a neighborhood a community? Students will explore what makes up a neighborhood; the important places, people, and feelings that come from their own neighborhoods. Exploring ideas of the individual elements that make up a neighborhood, to what makes a home and the meaning of community, students will create artworks using a variety of materials and artstic processes.

Goals

Concepts:
  • Students will understand the different components that make up a neighborhood
  • Students will understand the different components that make up a community •Student will understand the difference between a neighborhood and a community
  • Students will be able to visually express their emotions and feelings
  • Students will be able to relate their own personal experiences with the artworks they are creating
  • Students will understand the connection between their own artworks and artist exemplars from the past and present
Skills:
  • Students will understand how to compose, construct, and finish an artwork
  • Student will understand artistic processes such as printmaking, observational drawing, collage, sculpture, and painting
  • Students will understand that art is made up of shapes, lines, and color.
Disposions:
  • Students will be able to work collaboratively
  • Students will be able to work individually
  • Students will be open to new experiences and sharing their own stories and emotions with the rest of the class

Weekly Projects and Skills Learned

Week 1: “O The Places We Will Go” making a collaborative map of a neighborhood.

Materials: construction paper, markers.

Skills: cutting, collage, drawing.

Concepts: collaboration, shape, line, idenyifying the elements that make up a neighborhood.

Week 2: “Neighborhood to Community Printmaking” creating an additive and subtractive print of what makes a neighborhood a community.

Materials: styrofoam, pencils, markers, white paper.

Skills: positive and negative printmaking techniques, drawing.

Concepts: the meaning of a community, identifying the elements of a community.

Week 3: “The Feel of Home” creating fabric collages of Home based on the feel (texture) of our home and the feel (emotions) for our home.

Materials: fabric, scissors, glue.

Skills: shape, collage, glueing.

Concepts: texture, emotions, personal meaning of home.

Week 4: “Halloween Paper Mache Pumpkins” creating a pumpkin from paper mache with an emotional facial expression. Through decoration for a holiday a neighborhood becomes more friendly and more like a community.

Materials: newspaper, plastic bags, paper mache paste, tissue paper, pipe cleaners.

Skills: sculpture, paper mache, creating a 3d shape.

Concepts: showing emotion with facial expressions.

Week 5: “Building My Own Logo” writing community slogans to be placed on a constructed miniature fence.

Materials: popsicle sticks, paint/markers, glue, paper.

Skills: sculpture building a fence, construction.

Concepts: writing a message to impact/be]er the community it is seen in, uniting a community, presenting your ideas to the public.

Week 6: “Water Color Reliefs” observation drawing from objects that remind students of their communities.

Materials: paper, white crayon, pencils, water color, brushes.

Skills: observation drawing, relief, watercolor painting.

Concepts: giving meaning to an object.

Week 7: “Recycled Story-Tellers” creating sculptures of a story teller animal/robot/person out of recycled materials telling a story of why we should recycle and respect our environment.

Materials: recyclables, glue, tape, paint.

Skills: construction, sculpture.

Concepts: historical story tellers, recycling, narrative art, community/environment respect.

Week 8: “Community Building Design” design a building that would help your neighborhood become a community; what would be its purpose, who would use it, what would be in it?

Materials: paper, pencil, markers.

Skills: drawing, design.

Concepts: creating for a purpose, what are community needs.
